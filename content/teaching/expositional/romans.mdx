## About This Series

The Letter to the Romans is Paul's most systematic and comprehensive statement of the gospel. Written to a church he had not yet visited, it serves as both an introduction to his theology and a sustained argument for the righteousness of God revealed in Jesus Christ.

This series works through the letter verse by verse, with attention to the Greek text, the flow of Paul's argument, and the pastoral implications for the local church.

## Series Outline

### Part 1–3: The Problem (Romans 1–3)
Paul establishes that all humanity — Gentile and Jew alike — stands under the wrath of God. The diagnosis is total: all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God.

### Part 4–8: The Solution (Romans 3–5)
Justification by faith alone, apart from works of the law. The righteousness of God is given to the one who believes. Abraham is Paul's exhibit A.

### Part 9–14: The Implications (Romans 6–8)
Death to sin, life in the Spirit, freedom from condemnation. The Spirit-indwelt life is the life that fulfills what the law could not accomplish.

### Part 15–19: The Mystery (Romans 9–11)
The hardest and most contested section of the letter. God's sovereignty and human responsibility, Israel's unbelief and future hope, the doxology of 11:33–36.

### Part 20–24: The Ethics (Romans 12–16)
The renewed mind. The body of Christ. Conscience and weaker brothers. The gospel and the nations. Practical holiness in the local community.
