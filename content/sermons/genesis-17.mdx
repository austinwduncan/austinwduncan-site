## Thirteen Years of Silence

Between Genesis 16 and Genesis 17 lies thirteen years. The text offers no account of those years. Ishmael has been born and grown. Hagar remains in the household. And God, apparently, has been silent.

We should sit with this. The God who speaks, who makes covenants, who calls, who promises — is sometimes silent for long stretches. This is part of the texture of life with God.

When God speaks again, he begins with a self-identification: "I am God Almighty" — in Hebrew, *El Shaddai*. This name appears here for the first time. It emphasizes sufficiency, abundance, the God who is more than enough. The silence has not diminished him.

Then the command: "Walk before me, and be blameless."

## The Weight of a New Name

God's covenant speech in verses 3–8 is stunning in its scope. But the detail that changes everything is simple: *No longer shall your name be called Abram, but your name shall be Abraham.*

Names in the ancient world were not merely identifiers. They were definitions. They carried the story of who a person was.

Abram means "exalted father." A fine name — but a name about the past, about family origin.

Abraham means "father of a multitude." This is a name about a future that has not yet arrived. At 99 years old, with one son by a servant woman, Abraham is now walking around with a name that announces to everyone he meets: *I am the father of a great multitude.*

Every time someone calls his name, the promise is proclaimed. Every time he introduces himself, he is confessing faith.

## Identity Before Reality

This is the pattern of God's work: he declares us to be what we are not yet, so that we can become it.

He does not wait for Abraham to accumulate the offspring before he gives him the name. He gives him the name as a sign of what is coming, and calls Abraham to walk into it.

The same logic operates in the New Testament. You are *saints* — holy ones — Paul writes to congregations full of people still struggling with sin. You are *new creations*. You have been *raised with Christ*.

None of this is descriptive in the sense of reporting current behavior. It is declarative. It announces what is true in Christ, ahead of its full visible manifestation.

The name is the promise, worn on the body, spoken at every encounter.

## Walking in the Name

What does it mean to live as Abraham? To hold a name that announces a future you cannot yet see?

It means speaking the promises of God over your own life — not as prosperity gospel manipulation, but as covenant faith. It means allowing what God has declared to shape how you walk today.

Walk before me. Be blameless. Not because you have arrived, but because you carry the name of the one who has declared you his.

The covenant is everlasting. The land is real. The offspring — greater than the stars, greater than the sand — is coming.

Walk into the name he has given you.
