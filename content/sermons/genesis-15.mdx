## After These Things

The chapter opens with a temporal marker we easily pass over: "After these things." After chapter 14 — after the battle of the kings, after Abram's remarkable refusal to take the king of Sodom's offer of wealth, after his encounter with Melchizedek.

After doing the right thing, Abram gets a vision. This is worth pausing on. God speaks to those who are walking faithfully, not only to those who are in crisis.

"Fear not, Abram, I am your shield; your reward shall be very great."

But Abram's response reveals what is under the surface: "O Lord GOD, what will you give me, for I continue childless?"

He has done the right thing outwardly, and inwardly he is aching. The promise of land and offspring has not materialized. The reward of chapter 14 feels hollow when the fundamental promise — a son, a people, a future — is still undelivered.

## Faith and Doubt Are Not Opposites

What we see in Abram here is not unbelief. It is believing trust that is struggling under the weight of delay. He names his honest question to God.

This is important. Too often we think faith means suppressing honest doubt. Abram brings his doubt directly to God, and God does not rebuke him. God responds.

He takes Abram outside. *Count the stars, if you are able to number them.* "So shall your offspring be."

And Abram believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness.

This is the great justification text of the Old Testament, the one Paul will quote in Romans and Galatians, the one James will qualify by pointing to its fruit. Righteousness reckoned on the basis of faith. Not on circumcision — that comes later. Not on the law — centuries away. On faith alone.

## The Covenant-Cutting Ceremony

God's next act is remarkable: he enters into a formal covenant with Abram using the rituals of the ancient Near Eastern world. Animals are cut in halves and the parties walk between them — a visual enactment of the covenant's cost. *May what happened to these animals happen to me if I break this covenant.*

Abram prepares the animals. Then a deep sleep falls on him.

And only God passes through the pieces.

This is the glory of unconditional covenant. Abram does not walk through. He cannot hold up his end. He is asleep. The smoking fire pot and flaming torch — symbols of God's presence — pass through alone.

God binds himself to his promise without Abram's participation.

The covenant does not rest on Abram's faithfulness. It rests on God's.

## What We Do in the Dark

Before the covenant-cutting, a great darkness came upon Abram. God told him something hard: his offspring will be sojourners in a land that is not theirs, afflicted for four hundred years.

The promise is real. But between the promise and the fulfillment is suffering. Four hundred years of it.

We do not wait from promise to fulfillment in ease. The interim is often darkness, affliction, uncertainty. The question is whether the covenant-keeping God is still God in the dark.

He is.

He passed through alone. Not because Abram earned nothing, but because Abram could guarantee nothing. The covenant's certainty rests in the one who made it.

Wait on him. He will not be put to shame.
