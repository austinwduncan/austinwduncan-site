## The Problem of the Unanswered Promise

There is a long stretch between God's promise and God's fulfillment. Abram and Sarai have been waiting more than a decade. God said he would make a great nation from Abram's offspring. Abram believed. But nothing has happened.

And here is where we meet Hagar.

She enters the story not as a hero, not even as a full person in the narrative's eyes — she is Sarai's servant. She is Egyptian. She is powerless. She has no voice. And in the ancient Near East, the silence around a servant woman's preferences in matters like these would have been total.

## Sarai's Plan

Sarai's proposal to Abram — that he take Hagar as a surrogate — was not unusual for the time. Similar arrangements appear in Mesopotamian legal texts from the same period. The human logic is sound: if God intends to fulfill his promise through Abram's seed, let us help him along.

But what's striking here is not the cultural practice. It's the silence of Abram. He heard from God in chapter 15. God cut a covenant with him. And now, when his wife proposes a plan that bypasses that covenant, Abram says nothing. He listens to his wife's voice.

The echo of Eden is unmistakable. Another man, another woman's voice, another decision that will unravel in ways neither of them foresee.

## Hagar in the Wilderness

When Hagar conceives, the power dynamics shift. She begins to look at her mistress with contempt — the Hebrew word implies she treated Sarai as lesser. Sarai's response is swift and severe. She afflicts Hagar. The word used is the same word used to describe Egypt's treatment of Israel in Exodus. This is not incidental.

Hagar flees into the wilderness.

She is alone, pregnant, without protection, without a plan. She is sitting beside a spring of water on the way to Shur — the road back to Egypt. She is going home. Or trying to.

And this is the moment the Lord shows up.

## El Roi — The God Who Sees

The Angel of the LORD finds Hagar. This is the first appearance of the Angel of the LORD in Scripture. It is significant that his first visit is not to Abram, the covenant-bearer. It is to Hagar — the outcast, the marginalized, the one fleeing.

He asks her where she has come from and where she is going. These are not questions of ignorance. They are the questions of a God who is drawing out a confession.

Hagar says: "I am fleeing from my mistress Sarai."

She names where she came from. She does not name where she is going — because she does not know.

The Angel's instructions are hard: return and submit. This is not a feel-good ending. God does not promise her that Sarai will be kind. He calls her back into a difficult situation.

But he gives her something.

He gives her a son. He gives him a name — Ishmael, meaning *God hears*. And he gives her a promise: her offspring will be multiplied. She will be the mother of a people.

And then Hagar does something no one else in Genesis does. She gives God a name.

*El Roi* — the God who sees me.

> "Truly here I have seen him who looks after me." (v. 13)

## What This Passage Means for Us

There are people in your life right now who feel unseen. There are people in your pew, in your family, on your street — running toward Egypt, toward anything familiar, because what they left behind was unbearable.

El Roi finds them in the wilderness.

This is the character of our God. He does not wait at the temple for the respectable. He goes into the wilderness to find the ones running away. He asks them where they've come from. He calls them by name. He sees them.

The gospel is the fullest expression of El Roi. The Son of God went into the wilderness — forty days, in the same kind of desolate place. He was tempted where we are tempted. He was seen by no one who cared. And from that wilderness, he came out to find us in ours.

You are not unseen. Even here. Even in this.
