The question of how to read the Old Testament is not new. It has occupied the church since the beginning, and the answers have varied widely. The early church fathers read Scripture with a freedom and creativity that would make many modern evangelical interpreters uncomfortable. Understanding what they were doing — and what the limits of their method are — is important for any serious engagement with biblical hermeneutics.

## The Alexandrian Approach

The Alexandrian school, associated with Origen and later with Cyril, read Scripture at multiple levels simultaneously. The "literal" sense was the surface meaning of the text. The "allegorical" sense disclosed the spiritual or doctrinal meaning hidden within the literal. The "moral" sense applied the text to the soul's journey. The "anagogical" sense pointed toward eschatological realities.

Origen's reading of the Exodus narrative is representative: the literal sense is the historical account of Israel's deliverance from Egypt. But the crossing of the Red Sea is also baptism. The manna is the Eucharist. The entry into Canaan is the soul's ascent to God. The literal and spiritual are simultaneously present, with the spiritual often regarded as more important.

The obvious danger here is that the allegorical method can be used to make the text say anything. Origen himself was eventually condemned (posthumously) in part because his allegorical flights produced theological novelties that the church regarded as dangerous.

## The Antiochene Critique

The Antiochene school — Theodore of Mopsuestia, John Chrysostom, Theodoret — pushed back against what they saw as the excesses of Alexandrian allegory. They emphasized the *theoria* (vision) of the text: the literal-historical sense was primary, and typological readings of the Old Testament must be grounded in genuine historical correspondence, not merely verbal parallels.

The Antiochenes were not anti-typological. They read the Old Testament as pointing toward Christ. But they insisted that the pointer must be genuinely planted in history — that the type must have real historical substance, not merely serve as a launching pad for speculation.

## Typology vs. Allegory

The contemporary consensus in evangelical hermeneutics tends to distinguish sharply between typology and allegory along these lines:

**Typology** is grounded in history. A type is a historical person, event, or institution that God designed to prefigure a later, greater reality. Adam is a type of Christ (Romans 5). The Passover lamb is a type of Christ (1 Corinthians 5:7). The tabernacle is a type of Christ's mediatorial work (Hebrews). The correspondence is intentional and providential — God embedded these structures in history precisely to point forward.

**Allegory** reads a text as a code where the surface meaning is largely irrelevant to the real meaning hidden beneath. The history doesn't matter; only the decoded spiritual content matters.

This distinction is useful but should not be drawn too sharply. Even typological readings require interpretive moves that go beyond the strict grammatical-historical meaning of the text. The question is always: how do we discern which correspondences are intended and which are imposed?

## A Chastened Appreciation

The Reformation recovered the literal-grammatical-historical sense of Scripture as primary — and this was necessary and right. The allegorical excesses of medieval interpretation had produced a church that could no longer hear what the text was actually saying.

But the post-Reformation evangelical tradition has sometimes overcorrected, producing an arid literalism that cannot see the forest of Christ for the trees of grammatical analysis.

The church fathers, for all their excesses, were asking the right question: how do these ancient texts speak to Christ? The New Testament itself — Matthew's use of Hosea, Hebrews's reading of the Levitical system, John's deployment of Exodus imagery — authorizes a reading of the Old Testament that is more than grammatical-historical in the narrow sense.

A mature hermeneutic will hold the plain sense of the text as the non-negotiable foundation, while remaining open to the typological dimensions that the New Testament itself shows us are present. We read the whole Bible as one book, with one Author, with a single redemptive storyline that centers on Jesus Christ.

The fathers were not wrong to read it this way. They were sometimes careless. There is a difference.
