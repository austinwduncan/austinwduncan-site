The average American now checks their phone 96 times per day. Every 10 minutes. The number has increased every year for the past decade, and there is no visible ceiling.

I am not interested in the productivity implications of this. I am interested in the theological ones.

## Attention Is Not Neutral

The ancient church understood that where we direct our attention is where we become. "We become what we behold," wrote William Blake, channeling something older than himself. The Desert Fathers developed elaborate disciplines of *nepsis* — watchfulness, sobriety of spirit — precisely because they understood that the ungoverned mind would be colonized by whatever presented itself most vividly and most frequently.

Augustine's *Confessions* is, among other things, an account of misdirected desire — of a man whose attention was captivated by lesser goods, whose love followed his gaze. His conversion was not only an intellectual event. It was a reordering of what he loved to look at.

The smartphone is not an attention-neutral tool. It has been designed, by teams of engineers with access to more psychological research than any previous generation of persuaders, to capture and hold attention as long as possible. The business model of most digital platforms is the monetization of sustained attention. They are very good at this.

## What Is Lost

The most important casualty of the attention economy is the capacity for sustained reading.

Not because people read less — arguably, we read more words per day than any previous generation. But scrolling is not reading. The kind of reading that has historically formed human beings — the slow, resistant, marginalia-producing encounter with a text that does not yield to a glance — this kind of reading is increasingly rare.

This matters for reasons beyond education. Long-form reading is a school of patience. The reader who stays with a difficult argument, who holds the thread of a complex narrative through many pages, who returns to a passage that resisted comprehension — this reader is developing a set of cognitive and moral capacities that are transferable. Patience. Tolerance of ambiguity. The ability to hold a thought across time.

Scripture is not designed for scrolling. It requires exactly the kind of reading that the attention economy is eroding.

## The Discipline of Reading as Spiritual Formation

I want to suggest that recovering the discipline of deep reading is not a cultural preference but a spiritual necessity for Christians in the present moment.

Not because reading is intrinsically holy. Not because the church cannot form people through oral culture — it did, for centuries, and in many parts of the world still does.

But because we live in a particular moment of saturation, in which the forces competing for our attention are more aggressive than anything in human history, and because the habits of attention required to read Scripture well are precisely the habits being most systematically undermined.

The recovery of reading is, in part, the recovery of the capacity to meet God in his Word.

## Practical Suggestions

Some things that have helped:

**Remove the phone from the bedroom.** The first and last inputs of the day shape the day. A phone beside the bed means the first act of the day is surrender to the feed.

**Read on paper when possible.** The tactile, linear, fixed nature of a physical book creates conditions for focus that screens resist.

**Read slowly.** Speed is a product of the efficiency culture, not the reading culture. A book read slowly and twice is worth more than ten books read quickly.

**Read hard things.** Difficulty is not the enemy; it is the point. The resistance of a demanding text is the weight training of the mind.

**Protect a daily reading hour.** Not a moment. An hour. Non-negotiable.

This is not asceticism for its own sake. It is the recognition that we are embodied creatures, that habits form souls, and that a person who cannot pay attention cannot pray.
