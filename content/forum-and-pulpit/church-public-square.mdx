The question is not new. Augustine wrestled with it after the sack of Rome. Luther and Zwingli disagreed about it sharply. The twentieth century produced both the church that capitulated to National Socialism and the church that resisted it — sometimes the same denomination, sometimes the same building.

The question has returned with new urgency. The public square has become more contested, more fragile, and more eager to conscript every institution in service of its preferred conclusions. The church is not exempt from this pressure. If anything, it is a particular target.

## The Temptation of Political Relevance

There is a version of faithful public engagement that is simply ambition wearing a clerical collar. The desire to be influential in the halls of power, to be quoted by the right people, to have access — this is not prophetic witness. It is patronage-seeking dressed up in prophetic language.

The prophets were not trying to be relevant. They were mostly trying to avoid being killed. Their public speech was not calculated for maximum political influence; it was spoken because they had no choice: "The LORD GOD has spoken; who can but prophesy?" (Amos 3:8). Their relevance was a byproduct of their faithfulness, not a strategy.

The church that tailors its message to maximize influence will lose both its message and its influence.

## The Temptation of Withdrawal

The opposite error is quietism — the conviction that the church should tend only its own garden and stay out of public life entirely. Let the world burn; the church's task is saving souls.

This is not the biblical picture. The prophets addressed kings and nations. Jesus taught about money, power, sex, and justice with a directness that alarmed every party in first-century Judaism. Paul's proclamation that "Jesus is Lord" in the Roman Empire was a public and political claim — *kyrios* was Caesar's title.

The church cannot be the church while refusing to speak to the realities of public life.

## The Shape of Prophetic Witness

What the church owes the public square is not a political program. It is not the chaplaincy of any party or movement. It is not the endorsement of candidates or the baptism of agendas.

What the church owes the public square is:

**Truth.** The church knows things that the public square cannot figure out on its own — things about human nature, about justice, about the nature of the good. Not everything, but something. It should say what it knows.

**Critique.** Every political order will eventually demand more than it deserves. The church's task is to say, clearly and without compromise, when Caesar is claiming what belongs to God. This will make the church unpopular with every party in succession. That is exactly as it should be.

**Presence.** The church's greatest contribution to the public square may not be its statements but its members — people of integrity, generosity, and genuine concern for the neighbor, scattered across every institution and vocation. Salt works by contact, not proclamation.

**Distinctiveness.** The church's most powerful witness to a fractured and partisan world is to be actually different — to be the place where enemies eat at the same table, where the powerful serve the weak, where truth is told and forgiveness is real. That witness requires that the church not be absorbed into the polarities of the age.

## What We Cannot Afford

We cannot afford to mistake partisan alignment for prophetic witness. We cannot afford to be indistinguishable from any political tribe. We cannot afford to be silent when the vulnerable are crushed. We cannot afford to be so loud about the policy that we have nothing left to say about the gospel.

The church owes the public square faithfulness. That is a harder gift to give than influence.
