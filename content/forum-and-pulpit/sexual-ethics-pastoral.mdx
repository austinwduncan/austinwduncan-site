The debate about Christian sexual ethics has been argued extensively at the level of biblical interpretation. I do not intend to re-litigate that debate here. The church's historic teaching — that sexual intimacy belongs within the covenant of marriage between a man and a woman — is well-attested in Scripture, has been the consistent witness of the church across traditions and centuries, and is held with conviction by the majority of the global church today.

What interests me is not the exegetical debate. It is the pastoral reality.

## The Gap Between Position and Practice

There is a significant gap between what many churches say they believe about sexual ethics and how they actually live together. Churches that hold the historic position can simultaneously be places where divorced people feel permanently stigmatized, where single adults are treated as peripheral, where people with same-sex attraction feel they must hide, and where anyone who has struggled sexually in the past carries shame that the gospel was supposed to remove.

This is not faithfulness. It is the worst of both worlds: the cost of the position without the fruit of the gospel.

The New Testament's vision of the church is of a community where the sexually broken (which is all of us, differently) find genuine belonging — not despite the body's ethical demands but through them. 1 Corinthians 6, which contains Paul's strongest statement about sexual immorality, also contains the extraordinary line: "And such were some of you. But you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ and by the Spirit of our God."

*Such were some of you.* The church at Corinth included people with complicated sexual histories. They were there. Paul writes to them as saints.

## What Pastoral Compassion Actually Requires

Pastoral compassion is not agreement. Compassion that never speaks honestly about the hard things is not compassion — it is flattery with warm feelings.

But pastoral compassion is also not the enforcement of standards from a safe distance. The pastor who holds the line on sexual ethics while having no meaningful relationships with gay people, with single adults longing for marriage, with divorced people still in pain, with those struggling with pornography in secret — this pastor is holding the line while missing the point.

Jesus ate with sinners. This was not accidental or incidental to his ministry. It was the form his ministry took. He was present to the people whose lives were complicated, whose sexual histories were public, whose shame was palpable. His presence was the gospel before he opened his mouth.

## Celibacy as Gift, Not Consolation Prize

One of the most important things the church can do is recover a genuine theology and practice of celibacy. In the New Testament, celibacy is not a lesser calling — it is a charism, a gift, a form of devotion that has a freedom marriage does not. Paul recommends it. Jesus honors it.

The church that treats singleness as a waiting room for marriage — where all of pastoral energy is organized around the married family unit — is not the church Paul describes. It is failing its single members, including those who are single for reasons of sexual orientation and those who are simply unmarried.

The church is a family. It is the family of God. Those who have no spouse or children are not incomplete members of this family. They are full members who may, in some cases, be more fully available to the community precisely because they are not divided in their attentions.

## Conviction and Belonging Together

The church I want to be part of holds the historic sexual ethic clearly, without apology and without embarrassment. It also holds the door open for everyone — including people whose lives do not match that ethic right now, people who are still figuring out what faithfulness looks like for them, people who are in the middle of something complicated.

This is not a contradiction. The church has always been this: a gathering of people who know they are not what they should be, sustained by a grace that is making them into something better, held together by the body and blood of the one who loved them before they had anything to offer.

Conviction and belonging are not in tension. They require each other.
