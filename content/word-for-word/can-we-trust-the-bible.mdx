This is the foundational question. Everything else in Christian theology stands or falls on the answer. If the Bible is not trustworthy, then we do not have reliable access to the gospel, to Jesus, to the nature of God, or to any of the claims Christians make about reality.

So the question deserves a serious answer, not a defensive one.

## The Manuscript Evidence

The New Testament is the most attested document from the ancient world — by an enormous margin. We have over 5,800 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament, compared to fewer than 700 manuscripts of Homer's *Iliad* (the next most attested ancient text), and only a handful of manuscripts for most classical works.

When there are variations in the manuscripts — and there are — the vast majority are trivial: spelling differences, word order, synonyms. Scholars estimate that the texts that are theologically significant and genuinely uncertain amount to less than 1% of the New Testament.

The gap between the original documents and our earliest existing manuscripts is also far shorter than for other ancient texts. For Caesar's *Gallic Wars*, the gap is about 900 years. For the New Testament, we have papyrus fragments within decades of composition.

## The Historical Reliability of the Gospels

The Gospels were written within the lifetime of eyewitnesses to the events they record. This is crucial. Ancient historiography was constrained by the same accountability we would expect today: living eyewitnesses could confirm or challenge the accounts.

The criterion of embarrassment is useful here: accounts that would embarrass the early church are more likely to be historical, because no one invents unflattering details. Women are the first witnesses to the resurrection — in a world where women's testimony was legally and culturally devalued. Peter denies Jesus three times. The disciples consistently misunderstand Jesus throughout the Gospels. These details are not propaganda; they are the texture of honest reporting.

## The Theological Claim

The historical and manuscript case supports the Bible's reliability. But the full Christian claim goes further: not just that the Bible is historically reliable, but that it is the inspired Word of God.

The doctrine of inspiration does not mean that God dictated the text as if the human authors were stenographers. It means that God worked through their personalities, their vocabularies, their historical situations, and their literary conventions to produce exactly what he intended to produce.

This is a theological claim that must be assessed on theological grounds — and the primary evidence is Jesus himself. Jesus treated the Old Testament as the authoritative Word of God. He quoted it, appealed to it, and submitted to it. If we trust Jesus, we have a reason to trust the Scriptures he trusted.

## Why It Matters

The question "can we trust the Bible?" is not an academic exercise. The Bible is the means by which we know who God is, what he has done in Christ, and what he requires of us. If it is unreliable, we have no gospel — only guesses.

The confidence Christians have in Scripture is not blind faith. It is faith that has examined the evidence and found it more than adequate.
