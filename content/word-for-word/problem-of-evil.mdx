The problem of evil is real. It is not a sophomore philosophy class trick question. It is the question behind every hospital room where a believer has prayed and not received the answer they asked for. It is the question behind every news cycle. It sits at the intersection of our belief in a good God and our experience of a broken world.

There are several versions of the problem. The *logical* problem asks whether God and evil can coexist at all — whether their simultaneous existence is a logical contradiction. The *evidential* problem is less absolute: it grants that God and evil could coexist, but argues that the *amount* and *kind* of evil in the world makes God's existence unlikely.

Both deserve careful answers. This is an attempt at one.

## The Logical Problem

The logical form of the argument runs like this:

1. If God is omnipotent, he can prevent all evil.
2. If God is omniscient, he knows about all evil.
3. If God is omnibenevolent, he wants to prevent all evil.
4. Evil exists.
5. Therefore, God does not exist.

This argument had serious currency for centuries. But most philosophers today — including atheist philosophers — consider it a failure. The argument assumes that an omnipotent, omniscient, omnibenevolent God could have *no morally sufficient reason* for permitting evil. But that assumption is false.

A morally sufficient reason exists if permitting evil produces a greater good that could not be achieved without it. Free will is the classic candidate: genuine freedom requires the ability to choose evil, and genuine love requires freedom. A world of moral automatons programmed to love would not be a world of love.

## The Evidential Problem

The evidential problem is harder and more honest. It does not claim to disprove God — only to make him less likely given the evidence. And the evidence, especially in cases of *natural* evil (suffering not caused by human choice — disease, earthquake, childhood cancer), is genuinely heavy.

Here the Christian response must be humble. We do not have access to all of God's reasons. The book of Job should calibrate our confidence: Job's friends were wrong not because their theology was heretical but because they applied it with too much certainty to a situation they did not fully understand.

What we can say is this: the Christian God is not a bystander to suffering. He entered it. The incarnation is God's answer to the problem of evil — not a logical rebuttal, but a costly personal response. He became mortal. He suffered unjustly. He died.

## The Cross as Answer

The cross does not explain why a child dies of cancer. It does not resolve the intellectual problem on a whiteboard. But it does tell us something about the kind of God we are dealing with.

The God who allows suffering is the God who entered suffering. He is not indifferent. He is not absent. He is not unacquainted with grief.

And the resurrection means that the last word about the world is not death and suffering. It is life. The cross and resurrection together make the claim: God is redeeming all of this, including the parts that make no sense right now.

## Living with the Question

For most people, the problem of evil is not a philosophical puzzle — it is a personal wound. They are not asking whether God exists. They are asking why *this* happened to *them*.

For that, the only honest answer is presence. We sit with Job's friends for a while — silent, alongside. And we point, eventually, to a Savior who said "my God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — who knows what dereliction feels like from the inside.

The question does not have a tidy answer. The faith that holds anyway is not naive. It is the faith of Job: "Though he slay me, I will hope in him."
